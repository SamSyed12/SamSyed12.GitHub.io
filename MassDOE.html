Insights: The Massachusetts' Educational Landscape

The Massachusetts Department of Education is a considerable contributor to the American educational endeavor. The department oversees a vast network of 1,800 schools across 410 districts, collectively educating nearly one million students. With a staggering annual expenditure exceeding $306 billion, understanding the dynamics within this educational ecosystem is crucial.

The dashboard below allows for a comprehensive analysis of the Massachusetts' education landscape by exploring department and pupil metrics and their relation to success and failures in the academic field. Using tableau, I transform this complex dataset into intuitive visual representations essential to revealing significant trends and correlations. Whether you are an educator, policymaker, or concerned citizen, this deep dive into the state's education system promises to reveal significant findings and spark meaningful conversations about the future of education in Massachusetts.

Some Key Takeaways

- Schools that feature greater percentages of high needs and economically disadvantaged students, consist of pupils that generally fare worse academically (at all stages of education and particularly on SAT tests). These schools tend to have a lower graduation %, lower rates of college attendance, and tend to feature a greater proportion of Hipsanic students enrolled in comparison to the average school in the state.

- Schools with a greater % of enrolled males have lower rates of graduation and college attendance, whilst also having higher drop out rates when compared to schools that are female dominant.

- Average class size, average employee salary, total expenditures , and average number of classes do not seem to significantly affect graduation rates, %drop out rates, and college attendance rates Some trends are noted but correlation tends to be weak and contrary to public perception, for example average class size is weakly negatively correlated with college attendance and total expenditure shows a weak negative correlation with graduation rates. with

- The districts of Lawrence, Chelsea, Springfield, Lowell , and Revere display some of the lowest rates of graduation and college attendance, have high rates of dropping out, and students in these districts generally perform worse on academic tests such as the SAT and MCAS.
